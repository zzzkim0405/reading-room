The Department of Astronomy Report

The department of astronomy released an urgent report in early winter.

A newly discovered cosmic object had begun to change trajectory in a gradual but unpredictable way.

Scientists called it Specimen-9, a rare interstellar specimen drifting between star systems.

The object was not just a rock—it seemed to contain traces of unknown fiction, as if reality itself had been slightly rewritten inside it.

Dr. Helen Ward, head of the research department, was asked to conduct the investigation.

Her calm temperament made her one of the most trusted scientists in the field.

She instructed her team to contemplate every possible explanation before drawing conclusions.

“This is not a moment for assumptions,” she said.

The policy of the international space agency required strict verification before any public announcement.

A policy like this existed to prevent panic—but also slowed response time.

Inside the observation chamber, the object floated in controlled vacuum conditions.

Its behavior was influenced by electromagnetic fields that acted like invisible architecture, shaping space around it.

One technician noted that even simple communication required precise pronoun usage in reports, to avoid ambiguity in multilingual data systems.

“It matters,” he said. “Even grammar affects interpretation.”

Dr. Ward nodded.

In scientific communication, clarity was part of conduct.

Both behavior and methodology were included in that term.

Outside the lab, a strong solar blow disturbed satellite signals, temporarily disrupting data transmission.

The object reacted.

It shifted slightly.

Not randomly—but as if responding.

“This is becoming increasingly conspicuous,” said one researcher.

The object’s internal structure began to reveal complex patterns, resembling biological architecture.

No one had seen anything like it.

At a press briefing, the agency attempted to maintain dignity.

“This discovery is under control,” the spokesperson said.

But journalists were skeptical.

One asked whether the situation was truly successful or if the data was being hidden.

Dr. Ward avoided speculation.

“We are still analyzing,” she replied.

Inside the lab, the object emitted faint radiation.

It was neither harmful nor stable.

A technician suggested an equipment upgrade to improve containment fields.

But another warned that intervention might accelerate changes.

“Is it advisable to interfere?” he asked.

No one answered immediately.

Meanwhile, Dr. Ward received instructions from higher authorities.

A new policy directive had arrived: limit disclosure, maintain public calm, and classify all findings.

She frowned.

“This is not purely scientific anymore,” she muttered.

It was political.

A department meeting was called.

Representatives debated whether to continue research or suspend it entirely.

Some argued for caution.

Others believed the discovery could lead to revolutionary breakthroughs in astronomy.

During the discussion, Dr. Ward stood and spoke clearly.

“We are not just observing a phenomenon. We are interacting with something that may redefine scientific fiction itself.”

Her words silenced the room.

Outside, solar winds continued to blow across the magnetosphere.

Inside containment, the object slowly stabilized—but not completely.

It seemed to contemplate its surroundings as much as scientists contemplated it.

One junior researcher whispered:

“It feels aware.”

Dr. Ward replied softly:

“Or we are simply projecting human temperament onto the unknown.”

The debate continued late into the night.

Eventually, a decision was made.

The research would continue—but under stricter policy control.

Public communication would be limited.

Only approved reports would be released.

Dr. Ward accepted the decision, though reluctantly.

As she left the chamber, she paused and looked back at the floating specimen.

For a moment, it seemed almost peaceful.

Almost alive.

And she wondered whether humanity was truly the observer—or merely part of a larger system it did not yet understand.

In the final line of the official report, one sentence was written:

*         Further observation is not only necessary—it is advisable.*

But no one was sure who had written it.
